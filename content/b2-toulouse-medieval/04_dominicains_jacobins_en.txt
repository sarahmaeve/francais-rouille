The Dominican Order and the Église des Jacobins

Characters:
- Isabelle — a tour guide and medieval historian


Isabelle : We are now in front of the Église des Jacobins, one of the most beautiful Gothic monuments in the south of France. But before we admire the architecture, let me explain why this place exists.

Isabelle : In 1215, a Castilian priest named Dominic de Guzmán arrived in Toulouse with a mission: to combat the Cathar heresy, not with weapons, but with words and example. Dominic had traveled through the Languedoc for years and he had understood that the Cathars attracted the people because they lived in poverty, unlike the Catholic prelates who displayed their wealth.

Isabelle : Dominic therefore founded an order of preaching friars — the Dominicans — who were to live in the same austerity as the Cathars while preaching Catholic doctrine. The order was officially approved by Pope Honorius III in 1216, right here in Toulouse. It is the first Dominican city in the world.

Isabelle : The friars first settled in a small church, then they built this magnificent convent starting in 1229 — the very year the Albigensian Crusade ended. The architecture you see reflects their ideal of simplicity: no ornamental sculptures, clean lines, brick.

Isabelle : But let's go inside. The wonder is found within: the famous "palm tree of the Jacobins." It is a single column twenty-eight meters tall whose ribs fan out at the top, like the branches of a palm tree. It is a masterpiece of southern Gothic art, of striking elegance.

Isabelle : There is also a remarkable detail: beneath the high altar rest the relics of Saint Thomas Aquinas, the greatest theologian of the medieval Church, who was himself a Dominican. His remains were transferred here in 1369, making this church a place of intellectual pilgrimage as much as spiritual.
