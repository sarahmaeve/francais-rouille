The Cerfa — Residence-Permit Application

An annotated form and a glossary of French paperwork

Here is a simplified representation of Cerfa number fifteen thousand one hundred and eighty-six, used for residence-permit applications. In France, a Cerfa is an official, numbered form that the administration requires for almost every procedure. The margin annotations explain the fields and the words you most often encounter on these forms.

Paperwork glossary

1 A Cerfa
An official, numbered administrative form used in nearly every procedure in France. The number and version (15186*03) tell you whether you have the right document.
2 Civil status ("état civil")
The set of information that identifies a person: name, given name(s), date and place of birth, sex, family status. The "civil-status service" is also a counter at the town hall.
3 Nationality / citizen ("ressortissant")
"Nationality" is your legal belonging to a country. A "ressortissant" of a country is a citizen of that country, as seen by the administration of another country.
4 Family status
A legal category: single, married, PACS partner, divorced, widow/widower. The PACS — Pacte civil de solidarité — is the French civil partnership. For the detailed conditions, especially when one or both partners are foreign, see the official F1618 sheet on service-public.fr (link at the bottom of the page).
5 Residence address
The address where you actually live in France. Note, it must be proven with a recent proof of address.
6 Reason for the application
The administrative reason you are requesting this document. "First application" for a new permit, "renewal" to extend one, "change of status" if your situation changes (studies → work, for example).
7 Supporting documents ("pièces justificatives")
The documents to attach to the file. The word is also used in the shorter form "un justificatif." Every French procedure begins with the question: "Which justificatifs do I need to bring?"
8 Proof of address
A document proving your current address: a recent bill (electricity, gas, water, internet, phone), a rent receipt, or an attestation of accommodation. It must be in your name and less than three months old.
9 Sworn translation
An official translation performed by a translator registered with a French court of appeal. It has legal value. An ordinary translation, however good, is not acceptable to the French administration.
10 Declaration on honour
A written commitment in which you certify that the information provided is accurate. Lying in a declaration on honour is a criminal offence. You'll meet this in many administrative files.

Other useful words

A receipt ("récépissé")
An official receipt proving your file has been submitted. It often serves as a temporary authorisation while the administration reviews your request.
An acknowledgement of receipt ("accusé de réception")
A confirmation that your letter or file has been received. Requested on registered mail with AR for important procedures.
An appointment slip ("convocation")
A document convening you to an administrative appointment on a specific date. Keep it carefully — you'll be asked for it at the door.
A valid ID document ("pièce d'identité en cours de validité")
A CNI, passport, or residence permit that has not expired. "En cours de validité" is the fixed formula.
A full copy ("copie intégrale")
A complete copy of a civil-status document, with all marginal annotations. Not to be confused with a simple extract.
