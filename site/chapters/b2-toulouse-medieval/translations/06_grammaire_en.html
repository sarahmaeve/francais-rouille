B2 Grammar: Narrating History

Grammar tools from the guided tour of Toulouse

In this chapter's dialogues, the guide Isabelle tells the history of medieval Toulouse. To narrate this story, she uses grammatical constructions that go beyond B1 level. Here are the two most important ones.

The Plus-que-parfait (Past Perfect)

The plus-que-parfait is the tense you use to talk about an action that happened before another past action. It's formed with the imparfait of avoir or être, plus the past participle.

For example, from our tour of Toulouse:

Dominique avait voyagé dans le sud de la France. Il avait compris que les méthodes de l'Église ne marchaient pas.

Dominic had traveled in the south of France. He had understood that the Church's methods weren't working.

Dominic had already traveled and had already understood — these actions were finished before he founded the Dominican order. That's the key difference: the plus-que-parfait places an action even further back in the past.

When you're telling a story in the past and need to flash back — "but before that, he had already done something" — that's the moment to use the plus-que-parfait. It's formed the same way as English "had + past participle."

Relative Pronouns dont and où

You already know qui (subject) and que (direct object). Here are two more relative pronouns, essential for building richer sentences.

Dont — replaces de + something

If the verb or expression uses de, use dont.

C'est une histoire dont il faut se souvenir.

It's a story that you must remember.
(= il faut se souvenir de cette histoire)

La ville dont les comtes régnaient sur un vaste territoire...

The city whose counts ruled over a vast territory...
(= les comtes de cette ville régnaient sur un vaste territoire)

Le mouvement religieux dont nous avons parlé...

The religious movement that we talked about...
(= nous avons parlé de ce mouvement)

The trick: look for the de. If you'd say "parler de" (to talk about), "se souvenir de" (to remember), "avoir besoin de" (to need), "avoir peur de" (to be afraid of) — then use dont. English has no direct equivalent; we'd say "about which," "whose," or just restructure the sentence.

Où — replaces a place or a moment in time

For places:

L'endroit où le taureau s'est arrêté est devenu sacré.

The place where the bull stopped became sacred.

La ville où les Dominicains se sont installés...

The city where the Dominicans settled...

For time:

L'époque où les Cathares prêchaient dans le sud...

The era when the Cathars preached in the south...

Le jour où Simon de Montfort a été tué...

The day when Simon de Montfort was killed...
