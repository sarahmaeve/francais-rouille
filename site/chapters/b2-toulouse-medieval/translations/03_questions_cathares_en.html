Questions About the Cathars

B2 — English Translation

Characters

  • Isabelle — a tour guide and medieval historian
  • Thomas — a French-speaking Canadian tourist
  • Émilie — a Belgian tourist
Thomas: Excuse me, Isabelle. You said the Cathars rejected the Catholic Church. But did they still consider themselves Christians?
Isabelle: Absolutely. The Cathars saw themselves as the true Christians, the direct heirs of the apostles. They read the Gospel of John with particular devotion and practiced a single sacrament they called the consolamentum — a laying on of hands that purified the soul.
Émilie: And the crusade, how long did it last exactly?
Isabelle: The Albigensian Crusade lasted twenty years, from 1209 to 1229. But the persecutions continued long after. The first great massacre took place at Béziers, in 1209, where the crusaders killed thousands of people — Cathars and Catholics alike. It is there that the papal legate Arnaud Amaury is attributed the terrible phrase: "Kill them all, God will recognize his own."
Thomas: That's horrifying. And Toulouse, was it attacked too?
Isabelle: Toulouse was besieged several times. Count Raymond VI was first forced to submit, then he revolted. In 1218, Simon de Montfort, the leader of the crusaders, was killed during the siege of Toulouse by a stone launched from the ramparts — probably by women who were defending the city.
Émilie: What happened to the Cathars after the crusade?
Isabelle: The Inquisition was created in 1233, precisely to hunt down the last Cathars. The last great burning took place at Montségur, in 1244, where more than two hundred Perfects chose to die rather than renounce their faith. Catharism gradually disappeared over the course of the 14th century.
Thomas: It's a tragic story. Thank you for telling it to us with such detail.
Isabelle: It's a story that must be known. It reminds us of the dangers of religious intolerance. Let's now go see a place directly connected to this history — the Église des Jacobins.
