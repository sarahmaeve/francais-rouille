# A Lesson on Occitan

**Characters:**
- **Madame Vidal** — a middle school teacher

**Madame Vidal:** Right, let's gather here, everyone. You see this bookstore? All the books here are in Occitan or about Occitan culture. And I know what you're thinking: "What's Occitan?" Well, that's exactly why we're here.

**Madame Vidal:** So, to start: Occitan is the language your great-grandparents spoke. Not all of them, but many of them. Here in Toulouse, just fifty years ago, people at the market spoke Occitan among themselves. It was the normal language, the language of everyday life.

**Madame Vidal:** And this language is much older than French. In the Middle Ages, Occitan was one of the most prestigious languages in Europe. The troubadours — you've heard about them in class, right? — they didn't write in French. They wrote in Occitan, in the "langue d'oc." In fact, that's where the name of our region comes from: Occitanie. The word comes directly from "langue d'oc."

**Madame Vidal:** But then, why don't we speak it anymore? That's a good question. First, there was the crusade against the Cathars in the 13th century. Northern France invaded the south, and the northern nobles replaced the southern ones. Those in power switched languages. Then, in 1539, King Francis I signed the Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts, which made French mandatory in all official documents. Occitan became a "lower" language — the language of peasants and workers.

**Madame Vidal:** And then there was the school system of the Republic. Yes, school — the kind you go to every day. From the 19th century on, teachers were tasked with making regional languages disappear. If a child spoke Occitan in class, they were punished. Sometimes they'd hang a wooden clog around their neck — it was called the "signal." Imagine being punished because you speak your family's language. That's exactly what happened here, in schools just like yours.

**Madame Vidal:** Today, things are different. Occitan is recognized as cultural heritage. There are bilingual schools — the calandretas — where children your age learn in both Occitan and French. There are musicians, writers, and artists who create in Occitan. And there are bookstores like this one that keep the culture alive.

**Madame Vidal:** So when you see the bilingual signs in the metro or on the streets, you'll now know that it's not just decoration. It's the memory of a language that was ours for centuries. And maybe some of you will want to learn it — that would be a wonderful way to reconnect with the history of your city. All right, let's go look at the books. And keep it down — we're in a bookstore!
