Voicemail — A User's Guide

A listening format in its own right, worth learning to tame

This chapter is a small laboratory. It gathers five voicemails, in five different contexts of everyday life in France. The format has its own rules, which it helps to know before diving in.

Voicemail — or répondeur, in everyday French — looks like dialogue, but it demands a very particular listening skill. The speaker can't see your face or catch your hesitation. There's no pause, no rewind, no "sorry, could you repeat that?". Everything goes past in one pass, often at natural speed, sometimes fast. Numbers, dates, and phone numbers are given once — and, if you're lucky, repeated at the end.

In real life, voicemail is everywhere. Your doctor's secretary reminds you that you have an appointment tomorrow. Your child's teacher asks you to call her back before school. Your plumber confirms he's coming in the afternoon. Your bank flags an unusual payment. A friend cancels dinner at the last minute. Knowing how to decode these messages is a genuine survival skill in France.

Tips for listening

  • Listen once without taking notes. Grab the context — who is speaking, why. You won't understand everything, and that's OK.
  • Listen a second time with a pencil. Note the time, the date, the phone number, the action requested. Those are the only things you can't guess from context.
  • French numbers are spoken in pairs. A mobile number like 06 17 53 82 64 is read out as zéro six, dix-sept, cinquante-trois, quatre-vingt-deux, soixante-quatre. Train your ear for pairs, not single digits.
  • Spot the register. A friendly message, "coucou ma belle, c'est moi," doesn't share the intonation of a bank message reminding you of a security rule. Register helps you anticipate the words that follow.
  • Look for the action expected of you. Almost every voicemail ends with a specific request: call back, confirm, send an SMS, log in to your online account. Find that request, and you've understood the essential.

The five messages that follow cover a medical practice, a primary school, a friend cancelling, a plumber, and a bank. Over to you.
