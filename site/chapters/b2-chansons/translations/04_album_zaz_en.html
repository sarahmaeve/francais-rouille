Zaz's Latest Album

B2 — English Translation

Characters

  • Matthieu — a French chanson enthusiast who has followed Zaz since her beginnings
  • Rebecca — an amateur musician who prefers Zaz's first album
Matthieu: Have you listened to "Sains et saufs" all the way through? I've had it on repeat all week. I'm completely hooked.
Rebecca: Yes, I listened to it twice. And… how should I put this… I'm a bit mixed on it.
Matthieu: Really? What didn't you like?
Rebecca: It's not that I didn't like it. It's just that… this isn't the Zaz I used to love. The first album, the one with "Je veux," was raw, it was street music with incredible energy. You could feel that this woman was singing from her gut. On "Sains et saufs," it's more restrained, more polished, more… tame.
Matthieu: But that's exactly what I like about it. She's matured. You can't ask an artist to make the same album for twenty years. "Je veux" had that street energy, sure, but you can't sing like that forever.
Rebecca: I'm not asking her to do the same thing again. But for example, "Mon cœur tu es fou" — it's pretty, sure, but it could be sung by anyone. That husky voice, that grit that made her unique — it's not there anymore. It's like she's mellowed out.
Matthieu: I disagree. Listen to "La flamme" — it's powerful, it's heartfelt, and her voice is still there. She doesn't belt anymore, fine, but she's learned to use nuance. That's what evolving means.
Rebecca: "La flamme" — that's true, that's my favorite track on the album. Along with "Au pays des merveilles" — that one has a sort of playful quality that recalls her early work.
Matthieu: See! There's a bit of everything on this album. And the duet with Raphaël at the end, "Une passerelle vers la mer" — it's gorgeous. Their two voices blend really well.
Rebecca: Yes, the duet works, I'll give you that. But don't you think that overall, the production is too slick? On her early albums, there were imperfections, sonic accidents, and that's what gave them character. Here, everything is smoothed out, mixed to perfection. It lacks spontaneity.
Matthieu: It's the first album she's released on tôt Ou tard. Maybe the label change influenced the artistic direction. But I see it as a natural evolution. Brel started out in cabarets too, and he ended up making orchestral albums. Nobody held it against him.
Rebecca: You're comparing Zaz to Brel now?
Matthieu: No, I'm drawing a parallel about artistic evolution. Every artist has the right to grow. And I'd rather have a Zaz who explores new territory than a Zaz who panders to fans by recycling "Je veux" endlessly.
Rebecca: OK, that's a fair argument. But don't you think she's lost part of her audience along the way? The people who discovered her through the street video might not see themselves in this music anymore.
Matthieu: Maybe, but she's gained others. And "Sains et saufs," the title — it means "safe and sound" — as if she's saying she's been through something and she's still here, still standing. There's an emotional maturity to it that I find really moving.
Rebecca: It's true that the lyrics are more personal. "Je pardonne," for example, is clearly autobiographical. And it's well written, I won't deny that.
Matthieu: There you go. So, final verdict? Are you keeping it in your playlist or not?
Rebecca: I'm keeping "La flamme," "Au pays des merveilles," and the duet with Raphaël. The rest, I'm less convinced. But I'm willing to listen again — sometimes the albums that don't grab you right away are the ones that age best.
Matthieu: That's exactly what I was going to say. Give it a chance, you'll see.
