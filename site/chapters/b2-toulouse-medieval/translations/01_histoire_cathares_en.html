Medieval Toulouse and the Cathars

B2 — English Translation

Characters

  • Isabelle — a tour guide and medieval historian
Isabelle: Welcome to Toulouse, the Pink City. We are here on the Place du Capitole, and I'm going to tell you the fascinating history of this city in the Middle Ages.
Isabelle: In the 12th century, Toulouse was one of the most important cities in Europe. The Counts of Toulouse ruled over a vast territory that stretched from the Garonne to Provence. The city enjoyed great autonomy, governed by its capitouls — magistrates elected by the city's bourgeoisie. It was a time of remarkable prosperity.
Isabelle: But Toulouse was also at the heart of a religious movement that would upend the entire region: Catharism. The Cathars — also called the "Good Men" or the "Perfects" — preached a form of Christianity radically different from that of Rome. They believed in a dualist world, where good and evil were in conflict. For them, the material world had been created by an evil force, and only the spirit was divine.
Isabelle: The Cathars rejected the hierarchy of the Catholic Church, its sacraments, and its wealth. They lived in poverty and simplicity. Their message appealed to many people, including nobles and bourgeois of the Languedoc. The Counts of Toulouse, without being Cathars themselves, tolerated this religion and refused to persecute their subjects.
Isabelle: This tolerance would provoke the wrath of Rome. In 1209, Pope Innocent III launched the Albigensian Crusade — the first crusade directed against Christians on Christian soil. The armies of northern France descended southward, and nothing would ever be the same for Toulouse.
