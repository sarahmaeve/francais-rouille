The Troubadours and Their Books

B2 — English Translation

Characters

  • Irene — an American tourist visiting Toulouse
  • Arnaud — the owner of the Librariá La Tuta d'Òc
Irene: So, these troubadours… are there books in French that explain their history well?
Arnaud: Yes, I have several. Here, this one is excellent — it's a bilingual anthology with the original poems in Old Occitan and the modern French translation on the facing page. It lets you see what the language looked like.
Irene: Old Occitan… is it very different from today's Occitan?
Arnaud: Somewhat, yes. Like Rabelais's French is different from modern French. But a current Occitan speaker can still understand the troubadour texts with a little effort. That's actually one of the great points of pride for Occitan enthusiasts: their medieval literature is directly accessible.
Irene: And the troubadours — did they all come from Toulouse?
Arnaud: No, not at all. They came from all over the Occitan territory — from Limousin, Aquitaine, Provence, Languedoc. The most famous, William IX of Aquitaine, was the Duke of Poitiers. But Toulouse was an important center. Count Raymond V held a brilliant court where troubadours were welcome.
Irene: And what did their poems talk about, exactly?
Arnaud: Mostly love — but not just any love. They invented what's called "fin'amor," courtly love. The poet would sing of his love for a noble lady, often unattainable. It was codified, refined, full of metaphors. It completely transformed the way Europe thought about love and the relationships between men and women.
Irene: That's fascinating. And were there female troubadours too?
Arnaud: Yes, they were called "trobairitz." We know of about twenty. The most famous is the Countess of Dia, who wrote poems of great emotional intensity. Here, I have a collection dedicated to the trobairitz — it just came out.
Irene: I'll take it! And do you have anything about the Albigensian Crusade and its impact on this culture?
Arnaud: Of course. This book here explains how the crusade destroyed not only the Cathars, but also the entire Occitan civilization that surrounded them. The noble courts disappeared, the troubadours lost their patrons, and the language began its long decline. It's a tragic story, but essential for understanding this region.
