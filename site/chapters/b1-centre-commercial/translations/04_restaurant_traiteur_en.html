Where to Eat? — The Traiteur and the Restaurant

B1 — English Translation

Characters

  • Alice — an American tourist tired after shopping
  • Paul — Alice's husband
  • Waiter — a waiter in a restaurant in the shopping center
Alice: Paul, I'm hungry. We've been shopping for two hours. Should we get something to eat?
Paul: Yeah, me too. What kind of restaurants are there in this mall?
Alice: There's a McDonald's, but we didn't cross the Atlantic to eat at McDonald's. Look, there's a traiteur over there. What exactly is a traiteur?
Paul: If I understand correctly, it's a shop that sells prepared dishes. Ready-made food, but good quality. You can buy a dish and eat it there or take it away.
Alice: Ah, kind of like a deli in the US?
Paul: Yes, sort of. But more refined. Look at the display — it looks delicious.
Alice: True. But I don't understand half the dishes. What's a parmentier de canard?
Paul: No idea. Should we ask?
Alice: No, wait. There's also a little restaurant right next door. We could sit down and order properly. My feet are killing me.
Paul: Good idea. Let's see if they have room.
Waiter: Hello, two of you? Would you like lunch?
Alice: Yes, please. Could we see the menu before we sit down?
Waiter: Of course, here's the menu. We also have a daily special today — it's a cod fillet with rice and vegetables, for thirteen euros.
Paul: Thanks. We'll have a look and let you know.
Alice: So, let's see… What are rillettes?
Paul: It's shredded meat, usually pork. You spread it on bread. It's a French specialty.
Alice: Ah, kind of like pulled pork as a pâté? That sounds good. And a croque-monsieur — we know that one, that's easy.
Paul: I'm going to have the daily special. Cabillaud is cod, right?
Waiter: Almost! It's the same fish, but in France we say cabillaud when it's fresh, and morue when it's dried and salted. Today's is nice and fresh — you'll see.
Alice: Good idea, the cod. It's light. And what do we get to drink?
Paul: A carafe of water — that's free here in France, right?
Alice: Yes, you can ask for a carafe of tap water. It's normal at restaurants. But I'd quite like a small glass of cider too — we're in Brittany after all.
Waiter: Have you decided?
Alice: Yes. For me, the rillettes as a starter and a croque-monsieur. And a glass of dry cider, please.
Paul: And for me, the daily special — the cod. And a carafe of water, please.
Waiter: Very good. Would you like bread with that?
Paul: Yes, please.
Waiter: Perfect, I'll bring everything out.
Alice: Thanks. Oh, and could we have the dessert menu afterwards?
Waiter: Of course, I'll bring it with your main courses.
