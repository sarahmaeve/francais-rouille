Here is a concrete example of a professional French email. Emails between French colleagues follow precise conventions: the subject line must be clear, "Cher" (Dear) is not used day-to-day, a simple "Bonjour" followed by the first name is enough between colleagues, and the signature is usually short and standardised.

French professional email conventions

  • The subject line should be explicit and dated if possible. "RTT request — dates" is far better than a vague "question". The recipient should be able to triage from the preview alone.
  • No "Cher Monsieur" between colleagues. A plain "Bonjour Ludo," or "Bonjour Ludo" is enough. "Cher" is reserved for clients, formal partners, or paper letters.
  • Tu or vous? It depends on the company culture. At Atelier Clairac, people use "tu". In a large bank or in public administration, it's more likely "vous". Watch your colleagues' emails before you commit.
  • The closing phrase. Between colleagues: "Bonne journée", "Belle journée", or "À bientôt". More formal: "Cordialement", "Bien cordialement". Very formal (clients, administration): "Je vous prie d'agréer... l'expression de mes salutations distinguées".
  • The signature stays short. First name, last name, job title, phone number. No need for a big logo or inspirational quote.
  • CC the people involved. Here, Maeve CCs Anaïs (HR), because HR tracks the RTT counters. It's a courtesy and it's useful.
  • Anticipate objections in the body of the email. Maeve makes clear she has briefed Raphaël and there are no pending deliverables. That shows she has thought about the impact, and it simplifies Ludo's reply.
