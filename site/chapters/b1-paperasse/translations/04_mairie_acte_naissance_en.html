At the Town Hall for a Birth Certificate

B1 — English Translation

Characters

  • Sabrina Fontaine — a civil-status officer at the Nantes town hall
  • Maeve Carrick — an American tenant preparing a PACS file with her partner Irene
Sabrina: Good morning, madam. Did you take a ticket?
Maeve: Good morning. Yes — I have number B forty-seven.
Sabrina: You're up, it's your turn. What can I do for you?
Maeve: So, I've come to ask some questions. I'd like to enter into a PACS with my partner here in Nantes, and I'd like to know which documents I need to bring to put the file together, because I'm American.
Sabrina: All right. For a PACS with a foreign partner, we ask for three main documents. First, your birth certificate — a full copy, less than three months old for the town hall. Second, a sworn translation of that certificate, if you have it in English. And third, a certificate of custom, which you obtain from your consulate or embassy.
Maeve: OK. So I do have my full birth certificate copy — my mother requested it for me in the US last week. But of course, it's in English.
Sabrina: Yes, that's normal. So it needs to be translated by a sworn translator registered with the court of appeal. This is very important: a regular translation is not enough. The translator has to be listed on the official roster of judicial experts.
Maeve: I see. And where can I find a sworn translator?
Sabrina: I can give you three names — they are on the list of the Rennes court of appeal, which is our attached court. Let me write them down for you. Ms. Lefort, in Nantes, twenty-five rue de la Fosse. Mr. Kennedy, in Rennes, also registered for English. And Ms. O'Connor, in Angers. All three translate English to French.
Maeve: And how much does it usually cost?
Sabrina: Rates vary by translator, but for a birth certificate, plan on between sixty and eighty euros. Sometimes a little more if the certificate is long. And allow about a week for the translation.
Maeve: OK. And the certificate of custom — what is that exactly?
Sabrina: It's a document issued by your embassy or consulate, certifying that you are free to enter into a PACS under American law. In short, it states that you are an adult, unmarried, and that there is no legal impediment to your PACS in your country of origin.
Maeve: All right. And I should contact the U.S. consulate in Paris for that?
Sabrina: Yes, exactly. The U.S. embassy in Paris issues this document. You usually have to allow two to three weeks. Remember to check on their website what documents they ask for on your side.
Maeve: OK. Once I have all that, how do I arrange the signing?
Sabrina: You make an appointment at the town hall, either online on the Nantes town hall website, or directly at the counter here. The signing takes place in a small room, in the presence of a civil-status officer, who is not us — it's another, specialised agent. You come with your partner, you sign the PACS agreement, and that's it.
Maeve: How long does the signing take?
Sabrina: About fifteen to twenty minutes. It's a simple formality, once the file is complete.
Maeve: And is there a waiting time to get an appointment?
Sabrina: At the moment, we have about a three-week wait. I'd recommend booking the appointment as soon as you start gathering the documents, so that the slot falls around the time you have everything ready.
Maeve: Great tip, I'll do that. One last question: do we need to bring anything on the day of the signing?
Sabrina: Yes — the ID documents of both of you, originals, of course. And the PACS agreement, which you will have drafted yourselves or downloaded from the Cerfa form. I can print the template for you, if you like.
Maeve: Yes, that would be kind, thank you.
Sabrina: Right, I'll print the PACS agreement Cerfa for you. You have two options: either use the standard agreement provided by the administration, or have a tailored agreement drafted by a notary. The standard agreement is free and largely sufficient in most cases.
Maeve: Perfect. I think we'll go with the standard one.
Sabrina: Very good. So, summary: one, full recent copy of your birth certificate; two, its sworn translation; three, certificate of custom from the consulate; four, both your ID documents; five, the completed PACS agreement. Once all of that is gathered, you book the appointment, you come together, you sign.
Maeve: Very clear. Thank you so much.
Sabrina: You're welcome. Here — the list of translators and the form. Good luck with the preparation, and congratulations on your plans.
Maeve: Thank you, that's very kind.
Sabrina: Good day, Ms. Carrick. Goodbye.
Maeve: Goodbye.
