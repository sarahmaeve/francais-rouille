# Occitan: Langue d'Oc, a Forgotten Language?

## From the golden age of the troubadours to the modern-day revival

In the Middle Ages, Occitan was no marginal language. It was the language of poetry, diplomacy, and commerce across all of southern Europe. The troubadours — those poet-musicians born in the noble courts of Languedoc and Aquitaine — invented lyric poetry in the vernacular, long before French or Italian became literary languages. Their courtly love songs, composed in *lenga d'òc*, influenced Dante, Petrarch, and all of European literature.

The distinction between the langue d'oc and the langue d'oïl dates back to the 13th century. Both referred to the way of saying "yes": *òc* in the south, *oïl* in the north. This was not simply a difference in pronunciation. Occitan and French were two distinct languages, each with its own grammar, vocabulary, and literature. A resident of 12th-century Toulouse would have understood a Parisian no better than a Spaniard.

The decline of Occitan began with the Albigensian Crusade in the 13th century, when the north militarily conquered the south. But it was the Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts, signed by King Francis I in 1539, that dealt the decisive blow: this decree made French the sole official language of administration and justice. Gradually, Occitan became the language of the countryside, the markets, and private life, while French took over education, politics, and public affairs.

The French Revolution accelerated this process. The revolutionaries viewed regional languages as obstacles to national unity. In the 19th century, children who spoke Occitan at school were punished — sometimes they'd have a wooden clog hung around their neck, the *signal*, to humiliate them in front of their classmates. Within two generations, the family transmission of the language had largely been broken.

Since the 1970s, an Occitan revival movement has been trying to reverse this trend. Bilingual schools — the *calandretas* — teach in Occitan from preschool onward. Bilingual signs are appearing on the streets of Toulouse, Montpellier, and Pau. Musicians like the Fabulous Trobadors blend Occitan with rap and reggae. The language is now recognized as cultural heritage by the French state, even though it does not have official language status. It is estimated that between 100,000 and 500,000 speakers remain today, mostly elderly — but the *calandretas* are training new young speakers every year who refuse to let the *lenga d'òc* die.
